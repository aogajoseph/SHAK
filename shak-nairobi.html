HISTORY OF SHAK NAIROBI

SPINABIFIDA and HYDROCEPHALUS ASSOCIATION of Kenya, SHAK - Nairobi is a Community Based Organization registered in 2013 under the Societies Act Cap 108 of the Laws of Kenya to promote health and wellness of persons with Spina bifida and Hydrocephalus through prevention, advocacy and publicity and lifelong care. The Organization was founded by a group of parents who felt the urge to come together and support one another in caring for children living with Spina bifida and Hydrocephalus.

SHAK began when Juliana Auma brought forth Phoebe Achieng' who was born with Spinabifida and Hydrocephalus. Back then, people knew very little about these conditions and as Juliana sought information and medical care from different hospitals, she landed at the Kijabe Mission hospital. Here she learnt a lot on Spina bifida and Hydrocephalus and started sharing her experiences with parents of children with similar conditions. They gradually gained popularity when she started inviting them to the Bethany Crippled Children’s Centre of Kenya (B.C.C.C.K) Kijabe, presently Cure International Hospital.

After some time, their numbers increased and they decided to start a parents' support group which would be registered as SHAK Nairobi CBO in 2013. When the late Dr. Richard Bransford, a missionary doctor then at B.C.C.C.K learned of their group, he informed the International Federation for Spinabifida and Hydrocephalus (IFSBH) who started funding their support group meetings. The funds were channeled through Bethany Kids (formed in 2004 when B.C.C.C.K became Cure International Hospital). The B.C.C.C.K through their mobile outreach clinics initiated similar support groups in their outreach sites. They were later named SHAK chapters. They were formed in this order: Nairobi, Mombasa, Eldoret, Naivasha, Thika, Kaplong, Embu, Nyahururu, Kisumu, Nakuru, Kitale, Machakos, Meru, Mwingi, Kisii and Narok.

These SHAK Chapters shared a medical background due to the challenges the members faced in bringing up children whose conditions required constant medical attention. However, as time went by, the Chapters evolved. Some into CBOs and others into self-help and disability lobby and advocacy groups to defend the human rights of persons with Spina bifida and Hydrocephalus. It was at this point that IFSBH realized the strength of these groups and advised them to form national entity that would unite the chapters. In 2003, SHAK Kenya was registered as an Association with the Registrar of Societies and became a member country of IFSBH.

SHAK Nairobi supports the community through Prevention, Early detection and Publicity and people with Spina bifida and Hydrocephalus through Early detection, Medical interventions, Advocacy and Lifelong care.

SHAK Kenya to date works directly with donors and has so far worked with 3 international partners i.e., IFSBH, Child-Help International and RHF and a few local partners e.g., National Council for Persons with Disabilities (N.C.P.W.D), Global Alliance for Improved Nutrition (G.A.I.N), County governments etc.

SHAK Nairobi CBO on the other hand has been thriving independently. Its members developed interests in learning more on Spina bifida and Hydrocephalus and gained motivation in caring for their children and supporting other families. SHAK Nairobi supports the community through Prevention, Early detection and Publicity and people with Spina bifida and Hydrocephalus through Early detection, Medical interventions, Advocacy and Lifelong care.

Today, SHAK Nairobi works with different partners locally and internationally to support people with Spina bifida and Hydrocephalus and the community at large. We provide lifelong care through the House of Hope project.
